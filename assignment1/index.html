Assignment 1 - Interative Experience Research Project

Ft. Amorphous - Strike

  • Two minute rundown experience

    • Upon entering the Amorphous website, it is blatantly obvious that this is a drawing website where users can use the various buttons to construct a monochromatic drawing. On the top right there are multiple 'tones' of brushes the users can choose from, ranging from plain white, to black and white spots, to plain black. This allows the users to produce shading to mimic the various light and shadows. Next to the tones, on the top, are the pen tool, eraser tool and bucket tool, each one clearly marked by an icon which makes the function unmistakable. and next to it, three more buttons that adjust the shape of the brush between a rectangle, square and circle. The buttons on and slider on the left allows the users to adjust the size of the brush, and there are undo, redo, move, and 'fit to screen' buttons as well. On the top left, the File and Image buttons, which drop down to show more options.

    I began interacting with it rather quickly and experimented a little bit with the brushes, both in terms of size, shape, and tones made available to me. As I was drawing with my mouse, and much more accustomed to drawing digitally on a tablet with a stylus pen, it was a bit difficult, however, it was for that reason that the undo button was such a godsend when trying to fix mistakes. I found myself adjusting the size of the brush often, and to my own surprise, I did not take full advantage of the available monochromatic tones to shade my drawing. Personally, I believe the reason for that was because I was unfamiliar with how to shade using textured tones, thus making them a bit intimidating, but over time I would warm up to it. Finally, I used the Save File function found under 'File' to save my drawing, as seen on the left.

  • What was the first thing you paid attention to when interacting with the experience?

    • When first interacting with Amorphous, I immediately started sliding the brush size slider to check the range of sizes the experience offered, trying to gauge how far I could go with my creativity.

  • What did you spend the most time engaging with?

    • The variety of brush tones was fun to play around with, as I spent a while swatching each one in the largest possible brush size to get a closer look at the textures. Besides that, I spent the most time drawing the actual illustration with the brush tool and fixing any mistakes with the undo button.

  • What was the most common action in your two minute interaction with the experience?

    • My most common action during the interaction was most definitely using the undo button to remove my mistakes. I also used the brush size slider often, but not as much as I did the undo button. I personally found it more reliable than the eraser tool.
  • What is your impression of the intended primary goal of the interactive experience?

    • The primary goal of the experience was not difficult at all to decipher as it was rather straightforward. It was quickly obvious that the goal is having the users attempt a new digital drawing experince.
  • How does the experience communicate it's primary goal?

    • The use of very icons and text within the website's buttons to help the users identify specific functions is clear and clean. They are simple, and very common, but that means that users will automatically know their functions through association.

  • What is your impression of the intended length of a single interaction and how often you are intended to interact with the experience?

    • I personally felt like the website itself was nothing special as an experience. Certainly it was unique due to the brushes having certain textures. It was simply a monochromatic, simplified version of Microsoft Paint with a few fun brushes. There are better alternatives to digital painting. This experience felt like something fun for a while, but not something for the long term, albeit I can see why users would come back again for the unique experience.

  • What metaphor or metaphors does the experience's mental model reference?

    • I believe Amorphous acts as a metaphor for a literal blank canvas, as well as a blackboard. The contrast between light and dark is also very harsh, the monochromatic nature of it reminding me of old black and white photography as well. The website could also serve as a metaphor for duality, binaries, and freedom of expression.

  • What does this reference suggest about how you should feel and/or act when engaging with it?

    • The metaphor of the blank canvas/blackboard incites the users to fill up the empty space with their own creative ideas. It picks on the human desire to build and create. The metaphor of duality and binaries, fuelled by the sharp contrast of black and white as the only available shades also allows the users to feel a sense of starkness, as it makes every single stroke show quite prominently against the black background. There is a feeling of finality, and one can't help but feel that their artwork has a great impact.
  • What is the most frustrating element of the interaction and what makes it frustrating to you?

    • I believe the most frustrating element of the website is how it depends on the limited number of tones available to the user. It takes quite a lot of experimenting and swatching to figure out how one might be able to use them in shading and texturing their works, and personally, I think it takes far too much effort to create a semi-decent drawing. In addition, since most people use computer mouses instead of drawing tablets and pens, it was also quite frustrating to draw with it. There was a high chance of hand cramps.

  • What is the most satisfying element of the interaction and what makes it satisfying to you?

    • It is a particularly satifying feeling when you need to fill in a large part of your drawing using the paintbucket tool.
